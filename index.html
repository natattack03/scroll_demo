What is March Madness?
March Madness is the colloquial name for the NCAA Division I Men's and Women's Basketball Tournaments.

This annual sports extravaganza captures the hearts of millions as 68 teams compete in a single-elimination format to win the national championship.

The drama, the suspense, and the sheer unpredictability make March Madness a highlight of the American sports calendar.
There are over a 1000 teams in the NCAA. Which teams get to play in March Madness?
Eligible teams are determined through bids.
What Are Bids?
Bids are invitations awarded to college basketball teams to participate in the March Madness tournament. There are two types of bids: automatic and at-large.
Automatic Bids
These are given to 32 teams that win their respective conference tournaments. Each of the 32 NCAA Division I conferences holds a championship tournament at the end of the regular season, and the winner of each receives a guaranteed spot in March Madness.
At-Large Bids
The NCAA Selection Committee, a group composed of conference commissioners and school athletic directors, awards at-large bids.

They select 36 teams that did not win their conference tournaments but are deemed worthy based on their overall season performance, strength of schedule, key wins, and other criteria.

This selection process happens on Selection Sunday, a day when the tournament bracket is revealed.
With the bids determined, the next question is: How is the actual tournament structured?

Transitioning from team selection to the heart of the competition, we now explore how the tournament is organized, setting the stage for a series of high-stakes rounds that captivate fans nationwide.
What is a seed?
Each team is assigned a "seed" based on their expected performance, which is determined by their record during the regular season and other factors considered by the Selection Committee.
Seeds range from 1 to 16 in each of the four regions, with a seed of 1 being given to the teams deemed strongest.

This seeding helps to organize the tournament's matchups in a way that initially favors higher-seeded teams, theoretically allowing the best teams a better chance to progress in the competition.
Now, lets talk about the tournament itself!

March Madness unfolds in a series of high-stakes rounds, with each carrying the weight of elimination.
The tournament begins with the lowest-seeded teams (typically seeds 16 and some 11 or 12) competing to fill the final slots in the 64-team bracket.

This is known as the first four.
Teams face off within their regions in a knockout format known as the Regional Rounds, starting from the Second Round (often simply called the first round), progressing to the Sweet 16 and the Elite Eight. The structure of these rounds ensures that higher-seeded teams play lower-seeded ones in the initial games.
The winners from each of the four regions, each having survived several rounds of competition, advance to the national semifinals, which is celebrated as the Final Four.
Finally, it is time for the championship. This climactic game pits the two remaining teams against each other, concluding with the crowning of the national champion.
As the Final Four approaches and the championship looms, expectations are set for a predictable finale.

However, the essence of March Madness lies in its unpredictability. Just when the outcomes seem certain, the tournament surprises us--giants fall, and underdogs prevail. This is where the thrill of victory and the shock of defeat intertwine, setting the stage for the unexpected.
Now, let's explore the realm of upsets and Cinderella stories, where every match can turn the tables and every moment is potentially legendary.
Defining Upsets
In March Madness, an "upset" refers to a victory by a team seeded 5 or more lines below its defeated opponent according to the National Collegiate Athletics Association (NCAA). These unexpected victories are one of the tournament's most exciting features, as they demonstrate that on any given day, any team can triumph regardless of previous records or expectations.
